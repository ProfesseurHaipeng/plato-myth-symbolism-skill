# Interpretation sheet

For each myth, write five short blocks:

1. **Narrative facts:** who speaks, to whom, when, and what happens.
2. **Argument link:** what immediately precedes and follows the story.
3. **Conceptual vocabulary:** key Greek terms and translation choices.
4. **Competing readings:** literalist, allegorical, political, psychological, or literary readings as appropriate.
5. **Reception and limits:** later uses, anachronisms, and what the passage cannot establish.

Then ask: if the myth is an image used in an argument, what does the image make easier to think, and what might it conceal? This keeps creative adaptation productive without calling it Plato's doctrine.

Use the final response order: **textual record; dramatic and argumentative context; concepts; competing readings; limits; next learning step**. A reading is not a textual fact merely because it is familiar in modern culture.
